Project

Sweetness without the sugar tax on health

The world eats far more added sugar than is good for it. Brazzein offers a different kind of sweet — but nature makes too little of it. So we make our own.

The problem

Excess added sugar drives obesity, type-2 diabetes and dental disease. Yet the alternatives aren't clean wins:

  • Many artificial sweeteners carry a bitter aftertaste and lingering safety debates.
  • Natural sweet proteins exist, but most denature when heated — useless for cooking or baking.
  • Brazzein is heat-stable and natural, but the wild berry (Pentadiplandra brazzeana) can't be farmed at scale.

Our solution

Move brazzein out of the rainforest and into a fermenter. We express the protein in microbial hosts and tune it for yield, sweetness and stability.

  • Recombinant production in a food-relevant yeast chassis, with E. coli for rapid screening.
  • Codon- and signal-optimised constructs for efficient secretion.
  • A characterisation pipeline linking sequence changes to perceived sweetness and thermal stability.
Design

How we build a sweeter protein

Four design pillars carry the project from gene to tasting glass. Each is a place where biology meets food science.

Pillar 1

Chassis selection

Comparing a secreting yeast against an E. coli screening strain to balance yield, folding of the three disulphide bonds, and food-safety.

Pillar 2

Expression & secretion

Signal peptides and promoters chosen so the cell exports folded brazzein into the medium, simplifying purification.

Pillar 3

Sequence engineering

Targeted mutations at known sweetness-determining residues to probe — and ideally raise — perceived sweetness.

Pillar 4

Characterisation

SDS-PAGE, thermal melts and a structured sensory panel tie every construct back to a measurable sweetness and stability profile.

Model

Models that guide the bench

We use computation to choose what to build next, not just to explain what we built.

Three modelling threads run alongside the wet lab, each feeding a concrete decision.

Structure & folding

Modelling brazzein's three disulphide bonds to flag mutations that would destabilise the fold before we clone them.

Sweetness interface

Mapping the residues that contact the sweet-taste receptor (T1R2-T1R3) to prioritise sweetness-enhancing mutations.

Expression kinetics

A simple ODE model of induction and secretion to estimate optimal induction time and predict yield.

Human Practices

Who is this sweetener really for?

We spoke with food scientists, dietitians and people living with diabetes to understand whether a protein sweetener would actually fit real kitchens and real diets — not just a lab bench.

Their input reshaped our goals: heat-stability moved to the top of the list, and regulatory and labelling questions became part of the design conversation rather than an afterthought.

Safety

Built to be safe by design

We work only with non-pathogenic, well-characterised lab chassis under standard institutional biosafety oversight. No part of the project involves toxins, pathogens or environmental release.

Because brazzein is intended as a food-relevant protein, we consider allergenicity and food-safety frameworks early, and document handling and disposal in line with iGEM's safety rules.
